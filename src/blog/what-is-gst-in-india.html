Implemented on July 1, 2017, the Goods and Services Tax (GST) is a unified, indirect tax structure that replaced multiple indirect taxes in India, such as VAT, service tax, excise duty, and octroi. It was introduced under the slogan "One Nation, One Tax".

The Direct Benefits of GST

Before GST, India had a complex system where taxes cascaded—meaning users paid "tax on tax" as goods moved through supply chains. GST eliminated this cascading effect, making logistics smoother and tax structures uniform across different states.

The Components of GST

Depending on whether a transaction is within a single state or between different states, GST is divided into three distinct components:

  • CGST (Central Goods and Services Tax): Collected by the Central Government on transactions occurring within a single state.
  • SGST (State Goods and Services Tax): Collected by the State Government on transactions occurring within a single state.
  • IGST (Integrated Goods and Services Tax): Collected by the Central Government on transactions between different states. (The revenue is later shared with the destination state).

For example, if an item bought in Mumbai has an 18% GST rate, the tax is split: 9% goes to the Central Government (CGST) and 9% goes to the Maharashtra Government (SGST).

The Standard Tax Slabs

Different categories of goods and services are taxed under five primary slabs:

  1. 0% (Exempt): Essential food grains, fresh vegetables, milk, and basic items.
  2. 5%: Common items like sugar, tea, spices, and coal.
  3. 12%: Computers, processed foods, and mobile phones.
  4. 18%: Capital goods, industrial items, IT services, and restaurants.
  5. 28%: Luxury and demerit goods, such as cars, tobacco, and soft drinks.
